  Every day, I rely on Git — a tool that exists not because
  someone wanted to profit from it, but because someone chose
  to share it. To me, freedom means collaboration. That single
  word is why open source matters: software built openly gives
  every person the freedom to learn, adapt, and improve it
  without asking permission.

  The tool I would build and share freely is: a student
  resource manager for college projects. By sharing it under
  an open license, I would be doing what Linus Torvalds did
  with Git — turning a personal frustration into a gift that
  outlasts any single person or company. Standing on the
  shoulders of giants means inheriting their generosity.
  I choose to pass it forward.

  Open source is not just a licensing model. It is a statement
  that knowledge belongs to everyone. Every line of code shared
  freely is a brick in a foundation that the whole world builds
  on. I believe software should empower people, not lock them
  in. I believe transparency builds better tools than secrecy.
  And I believe the best way to learn is to read, modify, and
  share — just as the open source community has always done.

  This is my commitment: to use open tools, to credit the
  giants whose work I stand on, and one day, to contribute
  back to the community that gave me everything I needed to
  grow as a developer.
